High Red Blood Cells- An elevated RBC count can be caused by dehydration, living at high altitude, performance-enhancing drugs, smoking, chronic lung disease, chronic heart disease, polycythemia vera, or, in some cases, certain tumors.

Remedies: If a medical condition is causing a high red blood cell count, your doctor may recommend a procedure or medication to lower it. In a procedure called a phlebotomy, a health professional inserts a needle into your vein and drains blood through a tube into a bag or container.

Low Red Blood Cells- A low RBC count means the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood is reduced, a condition called anemia. Anemia has many potential causes, but in general, is caused either by reduced production of red cells in the bone marrow or by an increased loss of red cells due to bleeding or other factors.

Remedies: Eating foods rich in these five nutrients can help you improve your red blood cell levels:

Iron: red meat, such as beef, organ meat, such as kidney and liver, dark, leafy, green vegetables, such as spinach and kale, dried fruits, such as prunes and raisins, beans, legumes, egg yolks

Folic Acid: Adding certain B vitamins to your diet can also be beneficial. Foods high in vitamin B-9 (folic acid) include: enriched breads, enriched cereals, dark, leafy, green vegetables, such as spinach and kale, beans, lentils, peas, nuts

Vitamin B-12: Foods high in vitamin B-12 include: red meat, such as beef,fish, dairy products, such as milk and cheese,eggs

Copper: Copper intake does not directly result in RBC production, but it can help your RBCs access the iron they need to replicate. Foods high in copper include: poultry, shellfish, liver, beans, cherries, nuts

Vitamin A Foods rich in vitamin A include: dark, leafy green vegetables, such as spinach and kale, sweet potatoes, squash, carrots, red peppers, fruits, such as watermelon, grapefruit, and cantaloupe.

{% if all_report.wbc > 10000 %}
High White Blood Cells- An elevated white blood cell count (leukocytosis) can be a sign of infection, inflammatory conditions, tissue damage, allergies, or cancer, especially leukemia. Minor elevations in the WBC count can be seen after strenuous exercise, during periods of stress, and with pregnancy.

Remedies:
1. Wash your hands frequently to reduce the risk of infection.
2. Avoid potential allergens.
3. Do not smoke.
4. Practice good dental hygiene.
5. Visit your dentist every six months.
6. Take all medications as directed by your healthcare provider.
7. Find ways to reduce your stress levels, like mindfulness or meditation.
8. Seek treatment for anxiety or depression as needed.
{% elif all_report.wbc < 5000 %}
Low White Blood Cells- A low WBC count (leukopenia) can be seen with several bone marrow diseases, lymphoma, severe infections, enlargement of the liver and spleen, certain autoimmune diseases, HIV/AIDS, radiation therapy or chemotherapy, and with some medications (especially certain antibiotics). White blood cell counts that are very low can produce susceptibility to infections.

Remedies:To minimize the chances of certain diseases and a low white blood cell count:
1. Practice good hygiene. For instance, wash your hands after using the bathroom and before cooking.
2. Be careful around pets and other animals that might transmit infections.
3. Make sure foods are thoroughly cooked before eating them.
4. Stay away from people who have infections.
5. Practice safe sex; avoid drugs and sharing needles.

High Platelet Count- An elevated platelet count (thrombocytosis) can be seen with blood loss from surgery or trauma, hemolytic anemias, certain bone marrow disorders, infections, cancers, or spleen removal. Very high platelet counts can lead to excessive blood clotting.

Remedies:
Eat healthy foods- Choose a varied diet rich in whole grains, vegetables and fruits, and low in saturated fats. Try to avoid trans fats. Learn about portion control to maintain a normal weight.
Increase your physical activity- Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate physical activity a day. Take a brisk daily walk, ride your bike or swim laps.
Stop smoking- Smoking increases your risk of blood clots. If you smoke, take steps to stop

1. Eating more leafy greens.
2. Eating more fatty fish.
3. Increasing folate consumption.
4. Avoiding alcohol.
5. Eating more citrus.
6. Consuming more iron-rich foods.
7. Trying a chlorophyll supplement.
8. Avoiding vitamin E and fish oil supplements.

High Hemoglobin- Elevated hemoglobin levels can occur with conditions that chronically reduce the amount of oxygen in the blood, such as chronic lung or heart disease, smoking, or living at high altitude. Increased hemoglobin can also result from diseases that increase the number of red blood cells in the blood such as polycythemia vera, and various cancers.

Remedies:
Diet- The most impactful changes you can make for your blood sugar levels are dietary, specifically cutting back on or eliminating your sugar and starch consumption.
Exercise- Exercise naturally fires up your body's insulin activity. Insulin is the hormone that helps sugar leave your bloodstream and get into your cells to energize them.
Manage stress and mental health- When our bodies are stressed or anxious, our stress hormone - cortisol - takes over. Cortisol prepares us for fight or flight by giving us an energy boost, which in fact is a blood sugar level spike.

Increasing iron intake- A person with reduced levels of hemoglobin may benefit from eating more iron-rich foods. Iron works to boost the production of hemoglobin, which also helps to form more red blood cells.

Increasing folate intake- Folate is a type of vitamin B that plays an essential part in hemoglobin production. The body uses folate to produce heme, a component of hemoglobin that helps to carry oxygen.

Maximizing iron absorption- Foods rich in vitamin C, such as citrus fruits, strawberries, and leafy green vegetables, can boost the amount of iron absorbed. Taking a vitamin C supplement may also help.

Taking iron supplements- A doctor may advise a person with extremely low levels of hemoglobin to take iron supplements. The dosage will depend on a person's levels.

{% if all_report.rcd > 14.6 %}
High Red Cell Distribution- A high RDW indicates that there is a lot of variability in size in a persons red blood cells. This fact can be helpful in assessing the cause of anemia, especially when also taking the MCV into account. A high RDW along with a high MCV is seen in anemia due to vitamin B12 or folate deficiency, hemolytic anemia, liver disease, post chemotherapy, and with various bone marrow disorders. A high RDW along with a low MCV is seen in iron deficiency anemia and in people who have both sickle cell and beta-thalassemia. A high RDW with a normal MCV can be seen in very early B12, folate, or iron deficiency. This pattern of red cell indices can also occur with chronic liver disease, with various bone marrow disorders, and with the combination of iron deficiency and either folate or B12 deficiency.

Remedies:
If your test results indicate you have a chronic blood disorder, such as anemia, you may be put on a treatment plan to increase the amount of oxygen that your red blood cells can carry. Depending on your specific condition, your doctor may recommend iron supplements, medications, and/or changes in your diet
{% elif all_report.rcd < 11.6 %}
Low Red Cell Distribution- A RDW result in the low range is an unusual result, which is not thought to have any clinical significance.

Remedies:
Eat a healthy and nutritious diet in order to prevent nutrient deficiencies. It is important that your diet includes enough iron, folate, and vitamin B12 [8, 11, 12]. Correcting nutritional deficiencies can help improve red blood cell production and decrease RDW levels.
{% else %} HURRAY!! A RDW in the optimal range can be quite useful in evaluating the cause of a persons anemia. A normal RDW with a high MCV can be seen with liver disease, chemotherapy, or bone marrow disease. A normal RDW with a normal MCV in a person with anemia may be a sign of anemia of chronic disease or anemia due to blood loss. {% endif %}

High Mean Platelet Volume- A high MPV indicates that a person's platelets are larger than normal. This may occur with a number of conditions including various cancers, heart disease, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, and hyperthyroidism. It may also occur following a bleeding episode or any other condition in which many platelets have been recently consumed, and the bone marrow is working overtime to replace the used-up platelets with younger, larger platelets. Not all causes are serious, and your doctor is the best person to determine the cause of high results.About This Test

Remedies:
Address Underlying Conditions- A high MPV is often due to an underlying medical condition. The most important thing is to work with your doctor to find out what's causing your high MPV and to treat any underlying conditions!
Lose Weight- Diabetes and obesity are associated with higher MPV.
Check Vitamin D Levels- Check your vitamin D levels. Vitamin D deficiency can increase MPV
Stop Smoking
{% elif all_report.mpv < 7.5 %}
Low Mean Platelet Volume- A low MPV means that platelets are smaller than average. Usually, a low MPV does not have any particular significance. It can, however, occur with various bone marrow disorders, inflammatory bowel disease, or following chemotherapy.

Remedies:
Inflammatory Diseases- Some inflammatory diseases, such as rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), and familial Mediterranean fever have been associated with low MPV.
Impaired Bone Marrow Function- New platelets are not produced by the bone marrow, so older, smaller platelets make up a larger proportion of all the platelets in the bloodstream.
Chemotherapeutics- Drugs that kill cells or reduce their division, such as chemotherapy drugs, can decrease platelet production and MPV
Genetic Disorders- Some rare genetic clotting disorders have been associated with lower MPV
